In both previous simulations, total energy was dominated by the electromagnetic field (roughly an order of magnitude larger than $\vec \theta$ energy). This raises the question whether the observed decoupling of $\vec \theta$ depends on this dominance. It is not hard to imagine that without a strong, disordered radiation field overpowering NLSM dynamics, the outcome would be different.

Another question left open by the previous simulations is the ultimate fate of the effective photon mass. Does it fall all the way to zero, or does it level off at som finite value? There are also hints of long-lived structures in those simulations. What happens to those?

Finding the answers to these questions requires running longer simulations with different ratios of electromagnetic to $\vec \theta$ energy. For easier comparison, it is convenient to use the same initial configuration and just scale the $\vec B$ field without minimizing energy density all over again (as that would change shapes and values of both $\vec \theta$ and $\vec B$). This increases the deviation from static equilibrium and is therefore likely to make the initial evolution somewhat faster, so strictly speaking it yields a lower bound on the time required to reach a given degree of homogenization, $\vec \theta$ alignment or similar statistical measure.

Concentrating on the key graphs, this is what happens when the initial $\vec B$ is scaled by factors 0.23571 (for equal energy in $\vec \theta$ and $\vec B$), 0.1, 0.01 and 0.001, respectively:

${\vec B} \to 0.23571 {\vec B}$

This simulation took thirteen days with the usual iteration step of $10^{-3}$ lattice time units. Note how the swings in $\vec B$ energy mirror those in $\vec E$ energy. This is what you would expect to see in the presence of plasma oscillations, i.e. oscillating average separation between charges of opposite sign. $\vec E$ energy peaks at the turning points, when the charges are momentarily standing still at maximum separation, while $\vec B$ energy peaks when they are crossing at maximum speed. (Of course, in this case the charges are not carried by electrons and ions, but by $\vec \theta$.)

Also note the small jump in $\vec \theta$ and total energy at $t \simeq 200$. Integration becomes harder as electromagnetic dissipation of $\vec \theta$ configurations is reduced, but the symplectic algorithm manages to get back on track after minor events like this.

${\vec B} \to 0.1 {\vec B}$

This simulation was faster, only eight days, presumably due to the absence of events like the one encountered by the previous one. Note how $\vec \theta$ energy quickly drops to the same level as magnetic field energy, with the difference going to cross term and electric field energy. Either excess magnetic or $\vec \theta$ energy seems to drive the formation of charged configurations.

${\vec B} \to 0.01 {\vec B}$

As the electromagnetic field is reduced further, the going gets tougher again. This simulation took almost ten days.

${\vec B} \to 0.001 {\vec B}$

This is close to the limit for how much the electromagnetic field can be reduced without causing the integration to diverge (with this particular set of initial conditions ). Several temporary deviations from conservation of energy are visible. The simulation took eleven days.

The anticlimactic conclusion is that even very weak electromagnetic fields drive $\vec \theta$ toward $\theta_3$ and decoupling, just at a slower pace.
